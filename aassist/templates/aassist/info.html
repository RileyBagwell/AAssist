Alcohol information and safety

Educational content only. Not medical advice.

How alcohol works

Alcohol (ethanol) is a central nervous system depressant. After drinking, it is absorbed primarily in the small intestine and to a lesser extent in the stomach, entering the bloodstream and traveling to the brain. Its effects scale with blood alcohol concentration (BAC), typically measured as grams of alcohol per deciliter of blood.

  • Onset: Most effects begin within 10â€“30 minutes, faster on an empty stomach.
  • Metabolism: The liver breaks down most alcohol via alcohol dehydrogenase. Typical elimination is about 0.015% BAC per hour (varies by person).
  • Factors: Body mass, sex, genetics, food, medications, and drinking pace all affect BAC and impairment.
  • Impairment: Even low BAC can impair judgment, coordination, and reaction time.

What is a standard drink?

A standard drink is a way to compare different beverages by their pure alcohol content. In the U.S., one standard drink contains about 14 grams (0.6 fl oz) of pure alcohol.

Beverage Typical serving Approx. ABV Standard drinks
Beer 12 oz (355 mL) 5% ~1
Wine 5 oz (148 mL) 12% ~1
Distilled spirits 1.5 oz (44 mL) 40% (80 proof) ~1

ABV = alcohol by volume. Craft beers, fortified wines, and cocktails can contain more than one standard drink per serving.

Estimating BAC

BAC rises as drinks are consumed and falls as the body metabolizes alcohol. Simple estimators (like the Widmark formula) can give ballpark figures, but real BAC varies widely. The safest assumption is that you are impaired if you have been drinking.

  • Time lowers BAC slowly: roughly 0.015% per hour after drinking stops.
  • Coffee, cold showers, exercise do not speed up metabolism.
  • Never drive after drinking. Use a designated driver or rideshare.

Low-risk drinking guidelines

  • Set a limit before you start and pace with non-alcoholic drinks.
  • Eat before and during drinking; avoid drinking on an empty stomach.
  • Avoid mixing alcohol with sedatives, opioids, benzodiazepines, or other depressants.
  • Consider alcohol-free days each week; watch for increasing tolerance or reliance.

Safety tips

  • Know what you are drinking; measure spirits instead of free-pouring.
  • Alternate alcohol with water; aim for one glass of water per drink.
  • Look out for friends; do not leave anyone who is very intoxicated alone.
  • Store alcohol securely away from children and pets.

Alcohol poisoning: warning signs

If you see these signs, call emergency services immediately:

  • Unconsciousness or cannot be awakened
  • Slow, irregular, or stopped breathing; bluish, pale, or clammy skin
  • Seizures, vomiting while unconscious, or inability to stand
  • Very slow heart rate, severe confusion, or hypothermia

Do not leave the person alone; place on their side to reduce choking risk.

Interactions and special considerations

  • Alcohol can interact with many medications (e.g., painkillers, sleep aids, antidepressants).
  • Pregnancy: no known safe amount. If pregnant or trying to conceive, avoid alcohol.
  • Health conditions: liver disease, pancreatitis, heart conditions, or mental health concerns may be worsened by alcohol. Consult a clinician.

Need help?

  • Emergency: Call local emergency number if someone is in danger.
  • Support: Talk to your primary care clinician or local support services.

Resources vary by location; check your local health department for services.
